As a child, Frigg's parents had told stories of their own struggles to make ends meet, of the long days spent tending to their family farm. But it was Frigg's grandmother, an elderly woman with eyes as bright as the stars, who had instilled in her a sense of resilience and determination. "Frigg Berzina," she would say, when her granddaughter asked about the meaning of life, "it is not what you have that matters, but what you make of it."

As Frigg grew older, she learned to wield this mantra like a shield. She took on odd jobs around town – cleaning, babysitting, and even working as a waitress at the local diner – but never stayed in one place for too long. One day, while exploring the woods outside of Saratoga, Frigg stumbled upon an old, leather-bound book buried beneath a fern. As she opened it, a yellowed piece of paper slipped out, bearing a poem that read: "In twilight's hush, where shadows play / Seek the truth in darkness's sway."

Frigg Berzina felt a shiver run down her spine as she read the words aloud to herself, wondering if this was more than just a simple trinket. The poem seemed to speak directly to her own soul, reminding her that even in the darkest moments, there was always hope.

With the book clutched tightly in her hand, Frigg Berzina walked back into town, ready to face whatever lay ahead. For the first time in years, she felt a sense of belonging – as if she had finally found a place where her own story would be told, and heard by others.
