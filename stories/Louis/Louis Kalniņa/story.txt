They introduced him to the works of Latvia's national composer, Janis Rozentāls, which sparked Louis' interest in exploring the country's folk music traditions.

As he grew older, Louis became increasingly fascinated with the stories and legends that had been passed down through generations of Latvian families. He spent countless hours collecting tales of heroes and mythical creatures, which he would later use to create a comprehensive database of Latvia's folklore.

Louis' work took him to rural villages, where he would often spend days learning traditional dances from local women and boys. His eyes would light up as they taught him the intricacies of the polka or the kļava, a traditional Latvian dance that told stories of love and heartbreak.

One of Louis' most significant discoveries was the existence of a lost folk song, known only to a handful of people in rural Latvia. He spent months tracking down the song's origins, eventually uncovering its connection to an ancient pagan ritual. The discovery sparked a renewed interest in Latvia's pre-Christian heritage, and Louis' work helped to shed new light on this fascinating aspect of Latvian culture.

Years later, as Louis sat in his office, surrounded by stacks of dusty books and recordings, he couldn't help but feel a sense of pride and accomplishment. He had spent his life preserving the stories and traditions of Latvia's people, and it was clear that his work would have a lasting impact on the country's cultural landscape.
