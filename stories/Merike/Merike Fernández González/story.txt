Her parents, both anthropologists, encouraged her curiosity, sending her on expeditions to remote corners of the globe.

As she grew older, Merike's fascination with the unexplained only deepened. She pursued a degree in archaeology, determined to uncover the secrets of civilizations lost to time. Her research took her from the scorching deserts of Egypt to the mist-shrouded jungles of South America.

The intricately carved stone statue depicted a figure with striking features – high cheekbones, full lips, and piercing green eyes. It was as if the statue had been waiting for her all along.

Merike Fernández González spent countless hours studying the statue, running DNA tests, and consulting with colleagues from around the world. As news of her discovery spread, Merike became an unlikely celebrity in academic circles. Scholars and enthusiasts alike clamored for a glimpse of the statue, which had been dubbed "La Última Hija" (The Last Daughter) by local legend. Merike Fernández González found herself at the center of a maelstrom, torn between her desire to uncover the truth and her growing unease with the attention.

Despite the chaos surrounding her, Merike remained steadfast in her pursuit of knowledge.
