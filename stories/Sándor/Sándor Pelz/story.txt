As he grew older, Pelz's passion for art only deepened. He went on to study fine art at the Academy of Visual Arts, where he honed his skills and developed a unique style that blended traditional techniques with modern influences. His professors took notice of his talent and encouraged him to showcase his work in local galleries.

After completing his studies, Pelz set out into the world, determined to make a name for himself as an artist. He traveled extensively, soaking up inspiration from the cultures he encountered along the way. His artwork became increasingly diverse, reflecting his experiences and observations of the world around him.

As he worked, he felt a deep sense of connection to the land and its people.

Back in his studio, Pelz carefully crafted the drawings into a series of charcoal portraits, each one capturing the essence of the abandoned church. He titled the work "The Forgotten Sanctuary" and submitted it to a prestigious art competition.

Months passed, and Pelz anxiously waited for the results. Finally, the day arrived when he received an invitation to display his artwork at the very same gallery where he had shown his early pieces. Overjoyed by the news, Sándor László Péczeli celebrated long into the night with friends and family.
