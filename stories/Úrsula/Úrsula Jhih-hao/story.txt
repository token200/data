Úrsula Jhih-hao was a master weaver, renowned for her exquisite tapestries that told stories of ancient myths and legends. Born in the small village of T'aiwan, where her ancestors had lived for generations, Úrsula's talent for weaving was passed down through her family like a treasured heirloom.

As a child, Úrsula would spend hours watching her mother weave intricate patterns on her loom, listening with wide eyes as she recounted tales of the gods and goddesses that once roamed the land. When her mother fell ill, Úrsula took up the loom herself, determined to keep the family tradition alive.

Years went by, and Úrsula's skills improved with each passing day. She began to experiment with new techniques, incorporating traditional motifs into modern designs. Her tapestries became sought after by collectors from all over the world, who admired not only their beauty but also the rich cultural heritage they represented.

One day, a prominent collector offered Úrsula a lucrative contract: he wanted her to create a massive tapestry that would tell the story of Taiwan's history and mythology. Úrsula Jhih-hao was both thrilled and intimidated by the challenge. She spent months researching and planning, pouring all her energy into bringing the project to life.

The resulting tapestry was breathtaking – a sweeping narrative that spanned centuries and generations. It told the tale of the founding of Taiwan, the struggles and triumphs of its people, and the legends that had shaped their culture. When it was finished, Úrsula stepped back and gazed upon her masterpiece, feeling an overwhelming sense of pride and accomplishment.

The collector's offer had been worth it – Úrsula Jhih-hao's tapestry became a national treasure, cherished by the people of Taiwan and celebrated around the world.
