Born and raised in Almaty, Kazakhstan, she had always been fascinated by the unique flora that grew in the rugged terrain surrounding her hometown.

Selma's groundbreaking research on the genus "Sakuraea" - named after the iconic cherry blossom trees that bloomed in Kazakhstan during springtime - earned her international recognition and numerous awards. Her work took her to remote locations across Central Asia, where she encountered a variety of plant species unlike any others found anywhere else in the world.

One such species was the "Aktau Bloom," a rare and stunning flower that only bloomed once every decade under specific celestial conditions. Selma's tireless efforts to study this phenomenon led to a deeper understanding of its unique properties, which had the potential to revolutionize the field of botany.

As her reputation grew, so did the number of accolades she received. She was awarded the prestigious Golden Botanist Award for her outstanding contributions to the field, and invited to speak at conferences around the world.
