The two artists met briefly in 1489, but it was enough to spark a lifelong interest in Bartosz's work. He spent countless hours studying da Vinci's techniques and experimenting with new methods, often to great success.

Bartosz de' Medici's big break came in 1492 when he received a commission from the powerful Medicis to paint a fresco for their family church. With the help of his mentor, Domenico Ghirlandaio, Bartosz crafted an exquisite piece that showcased his unique style and skill. Despite his success, Bartosz de' Medici remained humble and dedicated to his craft. He continued to study and innovate, pushing the boundaries of what was possible with paint and brush. In 1502, he received a special honor from Pope Alexander VI, who appointed him Royal Painter to the Court of Florence.

Years later, as Bartosz looked back on his life's work, he felt immense pride in having followed his passion and made a lasting impact on the art world. His name became synonymous with excellence, and his works remained beloved for generations to come. The legacy of Bartosz de' Medici lived on, inspiring countless artists and cementing his place as one of the most talented figures of the Renaissance.
