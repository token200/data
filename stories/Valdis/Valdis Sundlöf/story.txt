Valdis went on to study folklore and mythology at Uppsala University, where she earned her Ph.D. in comparative mythology. Her groundbreaking dissertation, "The Evolution of Nordic Mythology," was met with critical acclaim and established her as a leading expert in the field.

Despite her success, Valdis remained humble and dedicated to her work. She spent years traveling through rural Sweden, collecting and recording stories from local communities. Her extensive knowledge of the land and its people allowed her to gain insight into the cultural practices and traditions that had been passed down through generations.

Valdis's research was not limited to written records alone. She also conducted fieldwork, participating in rituals and ceremonies to better understand their significance. This approach earned her both praise and criticism from colleagues, but Valdis remained committed to uncovering the truth behind these enigmatic stories.

One of Valdis's most notable discoveries was a previously unknown myth about the Norse goddess Freyja. Through her research, she shed new light on the goddess's role in ancient Scandinavian society, challenging previous interpretations and earning recognition as a leading authority on the subject.

Throughout her career, Valdis Sundlöf remained true to her passion for folklore and mythology.
