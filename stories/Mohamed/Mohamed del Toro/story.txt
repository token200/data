Mohamed del Toro stepped off the train and onto the worn platform, his eyes scanning the bustling streets of Puerto Vallarta. He had been traveling for days, and the smell of saltwater and fresh fish wafting from the nearby market was a welcome respite from the dry desert air he'd grown accustomed to.

As a young man, Mohamed had always felt like an outsider in his own family's village. His dark skin and sharp features set him apart from the lighter-skinned villagers, who seemed to blend seamlessly into the sandstone landscape. He had left home at 19, determined to make a name for himself as a fisherman.

Years of working on the open water had hardened Mohamed, but it was also given him a sense of freedom he'd never known before. He navigated the waves with ease, his hands moving with the practiced strokes of one who knew every inch of the sea.

Now, at 35, Mohamed owned his own small fleet of boats, employing a dozen men to help him haul in the day's catch. He was respected in the community for his fairness and generosity, always willing to lend a hand or share what little he had with those in need.

As he made his way through the crowded market, Mohamed spotted his childhood friend, Sofia, hawking her wares on a colorful stand. She flashed him a bright smile, and Mohamed couldn't help but feel a pang of nostalgia. He'd left Puerto Vallarta behind all those years ago, seeking adventure in the wider world.
