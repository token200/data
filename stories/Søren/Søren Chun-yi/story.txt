Born to a Danish mother and a Taiwanese father, he had grown up surrounded by the vibrant colors and sounds of Taipei. His mixed heritage had instilled in him a unique perspective on the world, one that he channeled into his art.

As Søren Chun-yi wandered through the streets, his eyes would scan the architecture for inspiration. He was drawn to the ornate details of traditional Taiwanese temples, the sleek lines of modern skyscrapers, and the intricate patterns of street vendors' umbrellas. His sketchbook was always by his side, filled with quick studies of the city's ever-changing landscape.

Søren Chun-yi's art was not just about capturing the visual beauty of Taipei; it was also a reflection of his own identity. Growing up as an outsider in both Denmark and Taiwan had given him a distinct perspective on the world. His artwork often explored themes of belonging, cultural identity, and the tension between tradition and modernity.

The intricate carvings and colorful frescoes on its walls caught his eye, and he quickly set up his easel to capture the scene.
