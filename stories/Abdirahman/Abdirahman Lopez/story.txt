As a teenager, he was constantly torn between two worlds: the boisterous celebrations of Somali culture and the anguished introspection of his own Mexican-American identity. His parents, though well-intentioned, often found themselves caught in the middle, unable to reconcile their own dual heritage with Abdirahman's growing sense of disconnection.

Abdirahman Lopez spent most of his days wandering the desolate corridors of Lincoln High School, lost in a sea of unfamiliar faces and confusing social hierarchies. He was an outsider in every sense – an awkward, gangly boy with an unnerving habit of bursting into impassioned speeches about the injustices he'd read about in History class.

It wasn't until he stumbled upon a flyer for the Eastside poetry collective that Abdirahman Lopez finally found his footing. Among the eclectic group of spoken word enthusiasts and social justice warriors, he discovered a community that celebrated his own unique blend of cultures and experiences.

His voice boomed through the cramped room, leaving the audience agog and disquieted.

For the first time in his life, Abdirahman Lopez felt seen, heard, and understood. The weight of his dual heritage began to lift, replaced by a sense of purpose and belonging.
