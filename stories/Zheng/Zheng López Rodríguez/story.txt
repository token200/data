Born to a Spanish father and a Chinese mother, Zheng's upbringing was a melting pot of cultures. He grew up in Madrid, where his fascination with art and history began. As a young boy, he spent hours poring over books and watching documentaries on famous artists, learning their techniques and secrets. His natural talent soon became apparent, and he began to hone his skills, eventually opening his own workshop.

Zheng's forgeries were sought after by collectors and museums worldwide. He had an uncanny ability to understand the emotions and intentions behind a piece of art, allowing him to recreate them with eerie precision. His clients didn't just want replicas; they wanted experiences. They wanted to feel like they were witnessing a masterpiece being born.

One day, Zheng received a commission from a prominent museum in Paris. They wanted him to create a forgery of Van Gogh's "Sunflowers." The painting was one of the most iconic works of art in history, and Zheng was both thrilled and intimidated by the challenge.

For months, he worked tirelessly, pouring his heart and soul into the creation. He studied every brushstroke, every color choice, every nuance that made the original so special. When he finally finished, he stepped back to admire his work, and for a moment, it felt like he was gazing at the real thing.

But as Zheng López Rodríguez delved deeper into the world of forgery, he began to question whether his art was truly worth the cost. Was creating replicas more valuable than creating something original? The line between authenticity and deception grew thinner with each passing day. Zheng's own identity became tangled in the threads of his forgeries, leaving him to wonder: who was he, really?

As he packed up his tools and closed his workshop for the last time, Zheng couldn't help but feel a sense of loss. He had given himself away, piece by piece, to the world of art forgery.
