As she grew older, Charrier's curiosity only deepened, leading her to study the forbidden arts and delving into the world of magic and mysticism. Despite her unconventional interests, Charrier was a brilliant student and quickly made a name for herself in the academic circles of Ashihara. It was during her time at Tokyo University that Charrier's reputation as a leading expert in Japanese mysticism began to spread far and wide. Her thesis on the symbolism of tengu wings in ancient Japanese art was widely acclaimed, and she was soon approached by scholars from around the world seeking her expertise on various aspects of Japanese spirituality.

As her fame grew, so did Charrier's sense of purpose. She began to see herself as a guardian of sorts, tasked with preserving the traditional knowledge and practices of Japan for future generations. And so, with her characteristic enthusiasm and dedication, Watanabe Charrier continued to explore the mysteries of the universe, ever hungry for new knowledge and insights that would help her fulfill her role as a keeper of the sacred traditions.

In the end, it was Charrier's unwavering commitment to her craft that earned her the respect and admiration of her peers, solidifying her place as one of Japan's leading authorities on mysticism and spirituality.
