His work on the theory of general relativity, as well as his research in nuclear physics, caught the attention of the scientific community worldwide.

Tamm's most notable contribution to science was the development of a new understanding of the behavior of light in certain materials. In 1946, he introduced the concept of "Tamm states," which explained how electrons can tunnel through energy barriers and absorb photons.

Throughout his career, Alwin Tamm received numerous accolades for his contributions to physics. He was awarded the Enrico Fermi Award in 1955 and was elected a Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences in 1960.

Despite facing personal hardships, including losing his brother in World War II, Tamm remained committed to his passion for science and education. He continued to mentor young scientists at Columbia University until his retirement in 1972.
